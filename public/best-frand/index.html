Let's go shopping!

“Let's go shopping!” I say to my closest friend. We are invited in the store by sliding doors. There are aisles aligned in perfectly rigid, parallel rows. Each labeled with a life purpose.

Patrons are directed to specific aisles and are advised that all they need is only there. My friend and I are assigned an aisle too. I analyze and wonder. If something is good for one, then why must it be best for all?

I rush to investigate. My friend worries for me as I diverge from them to another row. “Don't worry,” I say, “I will meet you at the end of the aisle.”

We are separate now and despite my initial excitement, I am scared and alone. To steer myself, I look for my shopping list, scraping the bottom of my pockets. I realize I never made one. Maybe my friend has it? I could easily check in with them.

Or I could decide for myself! I peruse the shelves and study the labels. Analyze the problems, possible solutions, the facts. I decide decisively, with clear clarity. What I need is missing from this aisle, so I go to another -- a simple choice when one has accepted that my assigned aisle is not enough.

People leer at me. I am confronted by a man, who criticizes my life, who has only been to one aisle. I study his face. I am here, calm and peaceful, with a friend nearby, and he is there, alone and sad, despite all the people on his side. I continue cheerfully and turn the corner.

To my surprise, my friend is waiting for me. They are giggling with a shopping cart overflowing. I smirk at them, having proven my original argument:

What is good for one may not be best for all.
